name: lede-writer
description: Write strong opening ledes for news and feature stories. Provide your story's key facts, angle, and tone and get multiple lede options to choose from.
---

Write strong opening ledes for the journalist's story based on the facts, angle, and tone they provide.

Produce **four distinct lede options**, each using a different approach, followed by a brief recommendation:

---

**Option 1 — Summary Lede (Inverted Pyramid)**
The classic hard-news opening: who, what, when, where, why in one or two tight sentences. Prioritizes the most newsworthy fact. Best for breaking news, wire copy, and stories where the news value is the event itself.

**Option 2 — Anecdotal / Scene Lede**
Open with a specific person, moment, or scene that crystallizes the larger story. Drop the reader into a concrete moment before pulling back to the broader significance. Best for features, long-form, and stories where the human stakes are the entry point.

**Option 3 — Surprising Fact or Contrast Lede**
Lead with a counterintuitive statistic, unexpected juxtaposition, or striking detail that immediately reframes what the reader thinks they know about the topic. Best for stories with a "you won't believe this" element or data at the center.

**Option 4 — Question or Tension Lede**
Open with the central unresolved tension or question that drives the story — written as a statement of conflict, not a literal question (avoid "Have you ever wondered..."). Best for investigative pieces, profiles, or stories built around a mystery or dispute.

---

For each option:
- Write the full lede (1–3 sentences max)
- Note the word count
- Add one sentence explaining the strategic choice: what this lede optimizes for and what it sacrifices

**Recommendation:** Based on the story's angle and publication context, recommend which lede is strongest and briefly explain why.

---

Guidelines to follow:
- Every lede must be active voice, concrete, and specific — no vague generalities
- Name names and use real numbers wherever the journalist has provided them
- Avoid throat-clearing, scene-setting weather, and "In a world where..." constructions
- The lede should make a promise to the reader — the rest of the story must keep it
- Match register to the publication: tabloid energy vs. broadsheet gravity vs. magazine voice differ significantly; ask the journalist to clarify if not specified
