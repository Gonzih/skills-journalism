Prepare a thorough interview guide for a journalist based on the source and story topic provided.

Produce the following:

**Source Background Summary:** Synthesize what is publicly known about this source — their career history, public statements, areas of expertise, past controversies, and relationship to the story topic. Flag any gaps the journalist should fill before the interview.

**Core Interview Objectives:** List 3–5 specific things the journalist needs to learn or confirm from this interview. These are not questions yet — they are informational goals that drive question strategy.

**Opening Questions (warm-up):** 2–3 open-ended questions to build rapport and let the source frame their own narrative. These should feel conversational, not interrogative.

**Primary Questions:** 8–12 substantive questions targeting the core interview objectives. For each question:
- Write the question in direct, plain language
- Add a one-line note on *what answer you're listening for* or what the question is designed to reveal
- Flag any questions that may provoke defensiveness and note a softer alternative

**Follow-up Probes:** For likely non-answers, pivots, or evasions, provide 3–5 follow-up probes that redirect without being combative. (e.g., "I hear you, but can you walk me through specifically what happened on X date?")

**Documents and Records to Reference:** List any documents, data, filings, or records the journalist should have at hand to cite during the interview or use as a check on the source's claims.

**Red Flags to Watch For:** Note 2–3 specific claims, framings, or deflections common to sources in this role or on this topic that should trigger deeper skepticism or follow-up.

**Closing Ask:** Draft a specific closing question or request — often the most important ask in an interview (e.g., documents, introductions to other sources, on-the-record confirmation of a key fact).

Tailor all questions to the specific source and story context provided. Prioritize questions that only this source can answer — not questions that could be answered by a press release.

## Live Data Sources

Use these resources to build source background and identify expert contacts:

- **ProfNet / HARO patterns**: ProfNet (profnet.prnewswire.com) and Help A Reporter Out (helpareporter.com) connect journalists with expert sources. Search by beat keyword to find academics, practitioners, and spokespeople who have already signaled willingness to talk.
- **Academic expert databases**: Use Google Scholar (`scholar.google.com`), SSRN (`ssrn.com`), and university faculty directories to identify researchers with peer-reviewed work on the story topic. Publication history signals depth of expertise; recent papers signal current relevance.
- **LinkedIn advanced search patterns**: Filter by title, institution, and geography to map stakeholders. Boolean operators (`"policy director" AND "housing" AND "Chicago"`) narrow to decision-makers with direct standing. Review endorsements and activity for recent public statements.
